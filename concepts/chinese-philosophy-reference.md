# Chinese Philosophy Reference

## Overview
Reference doc covering Confucian rectification of names, Mencius, Ming Jia, Daoism, Yin-yang, I-Ching, Qi, Neo-Confucianism, and the substance-vs-process comparison. The actual philosophy, not the system's application.

## Confucian Rectification of Names (正名 zhèngmíng)

Analects 13.3: "It would certainly be the rectification of names." Full chain: if names are not correct → language won't accord with things → affairs can't be carried out → rites and music won't flourish → punishments won't be appropriate → the people won't know where to put hand and foot. Not abstract linguistic theory — political and ethical. A ruler who does not rule justly does not deserve the NAME "ruler." Names must track actuality. Connection to nominalism: shares the core anxiety that names can float free of what they designate, but direction reversed — Confucius asks "does the person bearing this title actually embody its meaning?" Both insist names without grounding are dangerous fictions. In system-language terms, zhèngmíng is a name-discipline: titles stay admissible only when behavioral actuality keeps supporting them. Ritual (*li*) and propriety function as structure-maintaining disciplines: order is cultivated from within role-grounded practice, not imposed by arbitrary naming alone.

## Mencius and the Four Sprouts (四端 sì duān)

Human nature is innately good (性善 xìng shàn). Four sprouts: compassion → benevolence (仁), shame → righteousness (義), deference → propriety (禮), approval/disapproval → wisdom (智). Proof: child-at-the-well — anyone seeing a child about to fall into a well feels immediate alarm, not for social reward. Sprouts must be cultivated, not manufactured — moral development is GROWTH, not construction. Ox Mountain parable: constant logging stripped a mountain bare, making people think it was always barren. Connection to attractor basins: the four sprouts are innate dispositions that, given adequate conditions, naturally develop toward specific virtues. The virtue is the attractor; the sprout is the initial condition within its basin; cultivation is the trajectory.

## Míng Jiā (名家 School of Names)

Warring States period (479-221 BCE). Most direct Chinese analog to Western nominalism. Gongsun Long: "A white horse is not a horse" — "horse" names shape, "white" names color, "white horse" names both. What each picks out differs. "Separating hard from white": hardness and whiteness in a white stone are distinct and separable properties, not a unified substance. Hui Shi: perspectivalist. "Heaven is as low as earth, mountains are level with marshes" — how we carve reality depends on perspective and scale. A.C. Graham treated the Míng Jiā as engaged in precisely the rational discourse about language-reality relationships that Western tradition associates with nominalism.

## Daoist Philosophy

Laozi (Daodejing, Chapter 1): "The Dao that can be spoken is not the eternal Dao." Naming is necessary (creation requires differentiation) but distorting (the named Dao is not the real Dao). Language carves an undifferentiated totality into discrete objects — simultaneously how we navigate reality and how we lose contact with deeper unity. Zhuangzi: Qí wù lùn — all distinctions (right/wrong, self/other) are perspectival constructions. The sage "walks two roads," inhabiting conventional distinctions without mistaking them for ultimate reality. Wu wei: not passivity but action without forcing — zìrán (self-so-ness, spontaneity). Resonates with complexity theory's emergence. Daoist support here is process-first and anti-essential: naming is pragmatically necessary but never final, and transformation matters more than fixed substance.

## Yin-Yang as Process

NOT substances, NOT substance dualism. Relational, contextual, dynamic. Principles: mutual arising (yin and yang define each other), mutual transformation (yin becomes yang), dynamic equilibrium (continuous oscillation), contextual relativity (activity and substance are perspectives on the same process). Connection to feedback loops: structurally similar to negative/positive feedback regulation, but the page should keep that as a comparative support line rather than an exact identity claim. Systems biology scholarship explicitly maps yin-yang onto homeostatic and allostatic mechanisms.

## Correlative Thinking (通變 tongbian)

Chinese correlative thinking treats reality as a patterned web of mutually conditioning processes rather than a set of independent substances connected by external efficient causes. This is a support line for relation-first and process-first description, not a substitute for formal operator math. Best fit for this wiki: it helps explain why change, balance, and patterned co-variation can be taken as primary without importing a mechanistic A-causes-B ontology into every layer.

## I-Ching (易經)

Combinatorial system on binary logic: yin line (broken ⚋) = 0, yang line (solid ⚊) = 1. Trigram = 3 lines = 2³ = 8 possibilities. Hexagram = 6 lines = 2⁶ = 64 possibilities. Each hexagram = a situation-in-transformation, not a static object. 易 (yì) means "change." Does NOT describe 64 things — describes 64 situations-in-transformation. Moving lines indicate transition to another hexagram. Fundamental commitment: change is primary, permanence is derivative — exact inverse of Western substance metaphysics. Leibniz received the Fu Xi sequence from Jesuit Bouvet in 1703, recognized it as structurally identical to his binary number system.

## Qi (氣)

NOT "energy" in Western sense. The material-yet-processual substrate — simultaneously what things are made of and the activity by which they come to be. Zhang Zai (1020-1077): "When qi condenses, it becomes visible and takes shape. When it disperses, it becomes invisible and returns to the Great Void." No creation ex nihilo, no absolute annihilation — phase transitions of a single processual substrate. Connection to nominalism: qi undercuts the need for abstract universals. If reality is one continuous processual substrate, "kinds" are patterns of qi-condensation, not instantiations of Platonic forms. Connection to process philosophy: qi is intrinsically dynamic. Does not require external mover. Close to Whitehead's actual occasions and creativity as ultimate category.

## Neo-Confucianism

Zhu Xi (1130-1200): Lǐ (理, pattern/principle) — intelligible structure, coherence. Qì (氣, material force) — dynamic psychophysical stuff. Zhu Xi grants li ontological priority but insists li never exists apart from qi. Difference from Platonic dualism: li is IMMANENT — exists only within qi, not apart from it. Zhang Zai's monistic alternative: qi itself is inherently patterned and self-organizing. Wang Fu-chih pushes the same direction more strictly: pattern is not a second realm but the tendency of qi-process to organize in determinate ways. Wang Yangming (1472-1529): Unity of Knowledge and Action (知行合一). To truly know the good is already to do it — collapses theory/practice distinction, anticipates pragmatist epistemology. Later Neo-Confucian support is strongest where li remains strictly immanent to qi; once li is treated like a separable transcendent principle, the support value drops for this wiki.

## Comparative Philosophy: Substance vs Process

The deepest structural difference (Hall, Ames, Graham, Jullien): Western philosophy operates through substance/attribute metaphysics. Chinese philosophy never developed this framework — operates through correlative thinking: reality is a web of mutually conditioning processes. Western "problems of reconciliation" (how do substances interact?) vs Chinese "problems of distinction" (how do things emerge from unity?). Chinese philosophy never generated the Western universals debate because without substance/attribute metaphysics, the question "do universals exist independently?" does not naturally arise. Chinese "nominalism" is not a position within a universals debate — it is the absence of the framework that makes such debate seem necessary.
