# Nominalism: Philosophical Foundation

Date: 2026-04-05
Status: Reference doc — the actual philosophy, not the system's application of it

---

## What Nominalism Is

Nominalism says: only particular things exist. Universals, abstract objects,
categories, and kinds are names we give to groups of particulars — not real
things that exist independently of their instances.

The word comes from Latin *nomen* (name). The core claim: general terms like
"red" or "human" are just names — linguistic conventions — not signs pointing
to real abstract entities.

Two forms:
- **Nominalism about universals**: denies that universals exist. Two red
  apples are not red by sharing a third thing called "redness."
- **Nominalism about abstract entities**: denies numbers, propositions, sets,
  properties, types exist as abstract objects.

---

## The Nominalist vs Realist Debate

**What realists/Platonists claim:**
- Universals are real entities existing independently of human thought
- When two things are both "red," they literally share a single entity (redness)
- Abstract objects (numbers, properties) exist outside space and time
- The world has objective qualitative structure that is DISCOVERED, not invented
- Laws of nature GOVERN — they push the world around

**What nominalists deny:**
- There are no universals — only particular things and our ways of grouping them
- Similarity is a brute fact about particulars, not explained by a shared abstract entity
- General terms are just names applied to groups of resembling particulars
- Mathematical "objects" are fictions, constructs, or eliminable
- Laws are descriptions of regularities, not governing principles

## David Hume (1711-1776)

The most relevant philosopher for this system.

### Causation: constant conjunction, not necessary connection

We never observe the "power" by which one event produces another. What we
actually observe is:
1. Contiguity — cause and effect are adjacent in space and time
2. Temporal priority — cause precedes effect
3. Constant conjunction — events of type A always followed by type B

That is ALL we observe. The feeling of necessity is a psychological habit
projected onto the world, not a feature of the world itself. After repeated
experience of conjunction, the mind develops a propensity to expect the
effect when the cause appears. This propensity is the source of "necessary
connection" — an internal impression, not something in the world.

### Custom and habit as the basis of inference

"Whenever the repetition of any particular act or operation produces a
propensity to renew the same act or operation... we always say that this
propensity is the effect of Custom." (EHU 5.1.5)

Custom is "the great guide of human life" — not reason, not rational
insight, but accumulated habit. Custom alone "makes us expect for the
future, a similar train of events with those which have appeared in the
past." (EHU 5.1.6)

### The problem of induction

We cannot rationally justify the inference from past regularities to future
regularities. Any justification is either:
- Demonstrative (a priori) — but no logical contradiction in the future
  differing from the past
- Probable (based on past experience) — circular, assumes the principle
  it tries to prove

Inductive inference is grounded in custom/habit, not in reason.

### Personal identity: the bundle theory

No persisting "self" or soul underlying experience. Introspection finds only
"a bundle or collection of different perceptions, which succeed each other
with an inconceivable rapidity, and are in a perpetual flux and movement."
(Treatise 1.4.6)

No impression of a "self" as such. What we call identity over time is a
fiction produced by the mind's tendency to confuse resemblance and continuity
with identity.

### Abstract ideas: all ideas are particular

We cannot form genuinely abstract general ideas. When we think of "triangle,"
we think of some PARTICULAR triangle. This particular idea can function as
general because it is associated with a general term that activates a habit
of association. Generality is a feature of how we USE particular ideas, not
a feature of the ideas themselves.

## George Berkeley (1685-1753)

### Esse est percipi (to be is to be perceived)

Sensible objects have no existence distinct from being perceived. Ordinary
objects are collections of ideas — an "apple" is a collection of a particular
color, shape, taste, smell, and feel. None can exist unperceived, since they
are all ideas, and ideas exist only in a mind.

The world consists of two kinds of things: minds (which perceive) and ideas
(which are perceived). There is no third category of "matter."

### Objects exist only relative to a perceiver

"What are the forementioned objects but the things we perceive by sense, and
what do we perceive besides our own ideas or sensations; and is it not
plainly repugnant that any one of these or any combination of them should
exist unperceived?" (Principles, section 4)

### Critique of abstract ideas

You cannot form an image of a triangle that is "neither oblique, nor
rectangle, neither equilateral, equicrural, nor scalenon" — such an idea
is incoherent. A particular idea becomes general by being made to REPRESENT
all particular things of a certain sort, by virtue of how we use it.

## William of Ockham (c. 1287-1347)

### Universals do not exist as real entities

Only individual, particular things exist. General terms are concepts in the
mind that naturally signify many individual things. There is no extra entity
"humanity" existing alongside individual humans.

### Ockham's Razor

"Entities must not be multiplied beyond necessity." If you can explain
phenomena with fewer ontological commitments, do so. You do not need
universals to explain why things are similar.

---

## How the Three Relate

Common ground: all deny that abstract general entities have mind-independent
existence. All privilege particulars over universals. All are anti-Platonist.

The progression:
- Ockham strips away universals (keeps material substance)
- Berkeley strips away matter (keeps minds)
- Hume strips away even the self and causal necessity (keeps only perceptions)

Each is more radical than the last. Each strips away more of what was assumed
to be fundamental.

## Modern Nominalism

### Quine and Goodman (1947)

"We do not believe in abstract entities. No one supposes that abstract
entities exist in space-time; but we mean more than this. We renounce
them altogether."

Attempted to reconstruct mathematics using only concrete particular entities.
Quine later retreated (indispensability argument: math is needed for science,
so we're committed to its objects). Goodman stayed strict.

### Trope theory

Tropes are PARTICULAR properties — this redness of this apple, not
"redness" in general. Two red things each have their own redness-trope;
the tropes resemble each other but are numerically distinct. Rejects
universals while keeping a rich property ontology.

### Hartry Field

Science Without Numbers (1980): mathematics is not indispensable to science.
Physical theories can be reformulated without numbers or sets. Mathematics
is a useful fiction — a conservative extension of nominalistic physical theories.

## Nominalism and Statistics

If there are no governing LAWS (universals operating in nature), then what
science discovers are PATTERNS — regularities, correlations, constant
conjunctions. Statistics and probability are the natural language for
describing patterns without committing to underlying necessities.

A nominalist does not say "it is a law of nature that all copper conducts
electricity." They say "every observed piece of copper has conducted
electricity" — a statistical regularity, not a metaphysical necessity.

The Humean framework naturally leads to treating scientific knowledge as
probabilistic pattern-recognition rather than discovery of eternal truths.

## Nominalism and Laws of Nature

The regularity vs governing debate:

**Humean/regularity view (nominalist-compatible):**
Laws are just descriptions of regularities — patterns in what happens.
They do not govern or produce events. David Lewis's "Best System Account":
laws are the theorems of the simplest and strongest systematization of all
particular facts. The world is "a vast mosaic of local matters of particular
fact, just one little thing and then another."

**Anti-Humean/governing view (realist):**
Laws involve real relations between universals. The law that Fs cause Gs
is explained by a necessitation relation N(F,G) between universals F and G.

The nominalist gravitates toward the Humean view: laws as regularities,
not as governing principles.

---

## Nominalism and Empiricism

Natural allies. Empiricism: all knowledge comes from sensory experience.
Experience only presents particular things — this apple, that sunset.
We never experience a universal as such. If you take empiricism seriously
(as Hume does most radically), you arrive at nominalism: universals and
abstract objects are never experienced, so the empiricist has no grounds
for believing in them.

Empiricism provides the epistemology (how we know).
Nominalism provides the ontology (what there is).
Together: reality is particular, knowledge comes from encountering particulars.

---

## Nominalism and Anti-Essentialism

If there are no universals, then things do not have essences — no fixed
abstract nature that makes a thing what it is. A human is not human because
they instantiate the universal "humanity." They are just a particular thing
resembling other particular things we call "human."

Categories are conventional, mutable, context-dependent.
