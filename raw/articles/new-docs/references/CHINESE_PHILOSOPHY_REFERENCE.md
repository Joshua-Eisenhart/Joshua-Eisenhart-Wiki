## Confucian Rectification of Names (正名 zhèngmíng)

Analects 13.3: "It would certainly be the rectification of names."

The full chain: if names are not correct → language won't accord with
things → affairs can't be carried out → rites and music won't flourish →
punishments won't be appropriate → the people won't know where to put
hand and foot.

Not abstract linguistic theory — political and ethical. A ruler who does
not rule justly does not deserve the NAME "ruler." Names must track
actuality: behavior must match role-titles.

Connection to nominalism: shares the core anxiety that names can float
free of what they designate. But direction is reversed. Western nominalism
asks "do universals exist?" Confucius asks "does the person bearing this
title actually embody its meaning?" Both insist names without grounding
in actuality are dangerous fictions. Confucius treats it as ethical-
political; Western nominalists treat it as metaphysical.

## Mencius and the Four Sprouts (四端 sì duān)

Human nature is innately good (性善 xìng shàn). Evidence: four sprouts,
innate moral dispositions:

| Sprout (emotional response) | Virtue it develops into |
|---|---|
| Compassion/alarm (惻隱) | Benevolence (仁 rén) |
| Shame/disdain (羞惡) | Righteousness (義 yì) |
| Deference/respect (辭讓) | Propriety (禮 lǐ) |
| Approval/disapproval (是非) | Wisdom (智 zhì) |

Proof: child-at-the-well (2A6). Anyone seeing a child about to fall into
a well feels immediate alarm and compassion — not for social reward.
Spontaneous reaction reveals innate moral structure.

Agricultural metaphors: sprouts must be cultivated, not manufactured.
Moral development is GROWTH, not construction. You nurture what is
already there. Ox Mountain parable: constant logging stripped a mountain
bare, making people think it was always barren.

Connection to attractor basins: the four sprouts are innate dispositions
that, given adequate conditions, naturally develop toward specific virtues.
The virtue is the attractor; the sprout is the initial condition within
its basin; cultivation is the trajectory. Bad environments are perturbations
knocking the system out of the basin. Development is natural unfolding of
endogenous dynamics, not imposition of exogenous constraints.

## Míng Jiā (名家 School of Names)

Warring States period (479-221 BCE). Most direct Chinese analog to
Western nominalism.

### Gongsun Long (c. 320-250 BCE)

White Horse Dialogue (白馬論): "A white horse is not a horse." Argument:
"horse" names shape; "white" names color; "white horse" names both.
Since what "white horse" picks out differs from what "horse" picks out,
they are not the same.

"Separating hard from white" (離堅白): hardness and whiteness in a white
stone are distinct and separable properties, not a unified substance.

### Hui Shi (c. 370-310 BCE)

Perspectivalist. Ten Theses include: "Heaven is as low as earth, mountains
are level with marshes." Point: how we carve reality into categories
depends on perspective and scale. No single way of drawing distinctions
is fixed by nature.

A.C. Graham (Disputers of the Tao, 1989) treated the Míng Jiā as engaged
in precisely the rational discourse about language-reality relationships
that Western tradition associates with nominalism and logical analysis.

## Daoist Philosophy

### Laozi (Daodejing, Chapter 1)

"The Dao that can be spoken is not the eternal Dao. The name that can be
named is not the eternal name. Nameless is the origin of Heaven and Earth.
Named is the mother of the myriad things."

Naming is necessary (creation requires differentiation) but distorting
(the named Dao is not the real Dao). Language carves an undifferentiated
totality into discrete objects — simultaneously how we navigate reality
and how we lose contact with its deeper unity.

### Zhuangzi

Qí wù lùn (齊物論, equalizing things): all distinctions — right/wrong,
self/other — are perspectival constructions. The sage "walks two roads,"
inhabiting conventional distinctions without mistaking them for ultimate
reality.

Skill stories (Cook Ding, Wheelwright Bian) illustrate wú wéi (無為,
non-action): effortless action from perfect attunement to Dao, not
deliberate planning.

### Wu Wei and Spontaneous Order

Wu wei is not passivity. It is action without forcing — zìrán (自然,
self-so-ness, spontaneity). The sage ruler creates conditions for
spontaneous order rather than imposing structure. Resonates with
complexity theory's emergence — complex order from simple local
interactions without central planning.

## Yin-Yang as Process

NOT substances. NOT substance dualism. Relational, contextual, dynamic.
The same phenomenon can be yin in one relationship and yang in another.

Principles:
- Mutual arising: yin and yang define each other. Neither exists independently.
- Mutual transformation: yin becomes yang, yang becomes yin.
- Dynamic equilibrium: continuous oscillation, never static balance.
- Contextual relativity: activity (yang) and substance (yin) are perspectives
  on the same process.

Connection to feedback loops: yin-yang dynamic is structurally identical
to negative/positive feedback regulation. When yang increases beyond
threshold, it generates yin as counterbalance, and vice versa. Recent
systems biology scholarship explicitly maps yin-yang onto homeostatic
and allostatic mechanisms — the mathematical structure is the same.

## I-Ching (易經)

Combinatorial system on binary logic:
- Yin line (broken ⚋) = 0
- Yang line (solid ⚊) = 1
- Trigram = 3 lines = 2³ = 8 possibilities (Eight Trigrams / bāguà)
- Hexagram = 6 lines = 2⁶ = 64 possibilities

Each hexagram = a situation-in-transformation, not a static object.
易 (yì) means "change." The Xici states: "Generating and generating,
growing and growing, never ceasing."

Does NOT describe 64 things. Describes 64 situations-in-transformation.
Moving lines indicate transition to another hexagram. Fundamental
commitment: change is primary, permanence is derivative — exact inverse
of Western substance metaphysics.

Leibniz received the Fu Xi sequence from Jesuit Bouvet in 1703, recognized
it as structurally identical to his binary number system.

## Qi (氣)

NOT "energy" in Western sense. The material-yet-processual substrate —
simultaneously what things are made of and the activity by which they
come to be.

Zhang Zai (1020-1077): "When qi condenses, it becomes visible and takes
shape as things. When it disperses, it becomes invisible and returns to
the Great Void (太虛)." No creation ex nihilo, no absolute annihilation —
phase transitions of a single processual substrate.

Qi includes both matter and forces governing matter's interactions.
Dispersed = invisible, insubstantial. Condensed = solid form.

Connection to nominalism: qi undercuts the need for abstract universals.
If reality is one continuous processual substrate undergoing phase
transitions, "kinds" are patterns of qi-condensation, not instantiations
of Platonic forms.

Connection to process philosophy: qi is intrinsically dynamic. Does not
require external mover (unlike Aristotelian passive matter). Existence
is fundamentally shēng (生) — life, growth, vitality. Close to Whitehead's
actual occasions and creativity as ultimate category.

## Neo-Confucianism

### Zhu Xi (1130-1200): Lǐ-Qì Framework

- Lǐ (理, pattern/principle): intelligible structure, coherence, normative
  order. "The valuable, intelligible way things fit together."
- Qì (氣, material force): dynamic psychophysical stuff in which patterns
  inhere.

Zhu Xi grants li ontological priority: "Li exists before Heaven and Earth."
But insists li never exists apart from qi.

Difference from Platonic dualism: Platonic Forms exist in a separate
transcendent realm, ontologically independent of matter. Zhu Xi's li is
IMMANENT — exists only within qi, not apart from it. More like inherent
patterning of a process than a blueprint in a separate dimension.

Zhang Zai's monistic alternative: qi itself is inherently patterned and
self-organizing. Li is not a separate principle but emergent property of
qi dynamics.

### Wang Yangming (1472-1529): Unity of Knowledge and Action

知行合一 (zhī xíng hé yī). Against Zhu Xi's external investigation, Wang
argued li is discovered within the mind-heart (xīn). To truly know the
good is already to do it. If you "know" something is right but don't act,
you don't truly know. Collapses theory/practice distinction. Anticipates
pragmatist epistemology.

## Comparative Philosophy: Substance vs Process

The deepest structural difference (Hall, Ames, Graham, Jullien):

Western philosophy: substance/attribute metaphysics. Stable substances
bear attributes. Change is a problem to explain. Generates the universals
debate, mind-body problem, problem of induction.

Chinese philosophy: never developed this framework. Operates through
correlative thinking (Hall & Ames): reality is a web of mutually
conditioning processes, not independent substances bearing properties.

Key contrasts:
- Western "problems of reconciliation" (how do substances interact?)
  vs Chinese "problems of distinction" (how do things emerge from unity?)
- Causal thinking (A pushes B) vs correlative thinking (A and B co-arise
  in patterned relationship — gǎnyìng 感應, stimulus-response)
- Being (static, eternal) vs shēng 生 (life/growth/generation as
  fundamental character of existence)
- Transcendent principles (Forms, God, Laws outside the world)
  vs immanent patterning (li within qi, Dao within myriad things)

Chinese philosophy never generated the Western universals debate because
without substance/attribute metaphysics, the question "do universals
exist independently?" does not naturally arise. Chinese "nominalism" is
not a position within a universals debate — it is the absence of the
framework that makes such debate seem necessary.
