# Evolutionary Epistemology and Universal Darwinism: Formal Reference

Date: 2026-04-05
Status: Reference doc — the actual traditions, not the system's application

---

## Evolutionary Epistemology

Three programs:

**EEM (Evolution of Epistemological Mechanisms):** How cognitive capacities
evolved biologically. Key figure: Konrad Lorenz (1941, 1943). What is
ontogenetically a priori (innate) is phylogenetically a posteriori (evolved).
Our categories of space, time, causality are adaptations shaped by the
environment, not features of pure reason. They are fallible — selection only
weeds out the grossly maladaptive, not the subtly wrong.

**EET (Evolutionary Epistemology of Theories):** Evolutionary models as
descriptions of how knowledge grows. Popper and Campbell's territory.

**Third program:** Historical emergence of abstract entities (number, value)
that precede individual acquisition.

## Popper's Evolutionary Epistemology

Growth of knowledge is structurally identical to biological evolution:

- Conjectures = variation. Bold, unjustified hypotheses — not derived from
  observation (Popper rejected induction). Imaginative leaps.
- Refutations = selection. Severe critical tests. Survivors provisionally
  retained. Refutation is "a step forward that takes us nearer to truth."
- Falsifiability = demarcation. Scientific iff in principle falsifiable —
  forbids some observable state. Exposes to selective pressure.

Generalized to all problem-solving from amoeba to Einstein. The amoeba's
failed trial behaviors are refuted conjectures. Einstein's advantage: he
lets theories die in his stead.

The formula: P1 → TT → EE → P2
(Problem → Tentative Theory → Error Elimination → New Problem)
Knowledge never terminates. Each solution generates new problems.

## Campbell's BVSR (Blind Variation and Selective Retention, 1960)

Three components:

1. Blind Variation: candidates not pre-adapted to solution. "Blind" =
   emission independent of environmental conditions, uncorrelated with
   correct solution. Not literally random — just not guided by
   foreknowledge of the answer.

2. Selective Retention: some variants differentially retained because
   they fit environmental demands better.

3. Retention and Propagation: successful variants preserved and transmitted.

BVSR is a superordinate process — not limited to biology. Encompasses
perception, learning, creativity, cultural evolution, scientific discovery.
Biological natural selection is one instantiation.

Vicarious selectors: nested hierarchies where higher-level processes
(thought experiments) substitute for lower-level ones (physical trial
and error), each vicariously performing selection at lower cost.

## Universal Darwinism

Evolution as substrate-independent algorithm. Three conditions: variation,
selection, retention (heredity).

**Dawkins (1983):** Replicator/vehicle framework. Replicators (genes, memes)
are copied with fidelity, fecundity, longevity. Vehicles (organisms, minds)
are constructed by replicators to enhance propagation.

**Dennett (1995, Darwin's Dangerous Idea):** Natural selection as "substrate-
neutral, mindless algorithm." Three requirements: (1) pattern survives long
enough, (2) variation occurs, (3) selective preference exists. Called it
"the single best idea anyone has ever had."

**Hull:** Replicator/interactor distinction. Interactor is any entity that
interacts cohesively with environment causing differential replication.
More general than Dawkins' "vehicle."

Candidates for Darwinian evolution: genes, memes, theories, technologies,
neurons/synapses, words, programs, firms, antibodies, institutions, legal
systems, and speculatively quantum states or universes.

## Neutral Theory and Drift (Kimura, 1968)

At the molecular level, majority of evolutionary changes are NOT driven
by selection but by random genetic drift of selectively neutral mutations.

- Most non-deleterious mutations are neutral (no fitness effect)
- In finite populations, neutral alleles reach fixation by chance
- Rate of neutral molecular evolution = neutral mutation rate,
  independent of population size
- Compatible with phenotypic adaptation being driven by selection

Genetic drift: random fluctuation of allele frequencies from finite
population. Strongest in small populations. A real evolutionary force,
not merely noise.

Nearly neutral theory (Ohta): mutations with very small selective effects
(|s| < 1/2Ne) behave effectively as neutral. Population size determines
the boundary between selection and drift.

Neutral networks (Wagner): many genotypes produce the same phenotype,
forming connected networks in sequence space. Populations drift along
neutral networks without phenotypic change, arriving near different
fitness basins. Basin-hopping via neutrality, without crossing valleys.

Robustness and evolvability are linked through neutral network topology:
robust genotypes (deep in basin) connect via neutral networks to genotypes
near other basins, facilitating long-term evolvability.

## Evolvability

Capacity to generate adaptive heritable phenotypic variation.

**Modularity:** Semi-independent modules reduce pleiotropy. Mutations
localized, beneficial mutations more likely. Modular architectures evolve
spontaneously in modularly varying environments.

**Facilitated variation (Kirschner & Gerhart, 2005):** Conserved core
processes weakly linked, combinatorially versatile. Novel phenotypes from
rewiring existing robust components, not inventing new mechanisms. Three
properties: weak regulatory linkage, exploratory behavior, compartmentation.

**Developmental bias:** Development channels variation in directions more
likely to be functional. Not purely limiting — channels toward functional
variants because development itself is evolved.

## Major Transitions in Evolution (Maynard Smith & Szathmáry, 1995)

1. Replicating molecules → populations in compartments (protocells)
2. Independent replicators → chromosomes (linked)
3. RNA as gene+enzyme → DNA genes + protein enzymes (genetic code)
4. Prokaryotes → eukaryotes (endosymbiosis)
5. Asexual → sexual (recombination)
6. Single-celled → multicellular (differentiation)
7. Solitary → colonies/societies (eusociality)
8. Primate societies → human societies with language

Common pattern: previously independent entities lose ability to replicate
independently, become parts of larger whole. A RATCHET: once the transition
occurs, components cannot revert.

Two defining features:
1. Transition in individuality — new higher-level units of selection
2. Novel inheritance system — new ways of transmitting information

## Niche Construction (Odling-Smee, Laland, Feldman, 2003)

Organisms systematically modify their environments. Modifications change
selection pressures on the constructor and other species.

Reciprocal causation: organisms modify environments, modified environments
select organisms. Not one-way arrows.

Ecological inheritance: organisms inherit modified environments from parents
(beaver kit inherits dam). Second inheritance system alongside genetic.

Key implications:
- Systematic (non-random) bias on selection pressures
- Positive feedback loops (modification favors same traits)
- Time-lagged effects
- Co-evolutionary dynamics between organisms and constructed environments

## Extended Evolutionary Synthesis

What it adds beyond the Modern Synthesis:

1. Developmental bias: development channels variation, shares responsibility
   for direction of evolution
2. Phenotypic plasticity: plasticity can precede genetic accommodation
   (plasticity-first evolution inverts the standard sequence)
3. Inclusive inheritance: epigenetic, behavioral, ecological, symbolic
   inheritance beyond DNA (Jablonka and Lamb's four dimensions)
4. Niche construction: organisms co-direct their evolution
5. Evolvability: capacity to evolve is itself evolved
6. Multilevel selection: operates at gene, cell, organism, group levels

Core shift: organism as active participant constructing its own trajectory,
not passive object of selection. Reciprocal causation, not unidirectional.

## Constructor Theory (Deutsch & Marletto, 2013-2015)

Laws as statements about which tasks (physical transformations) are
possible vs impossible, and why. Counterfactual statements as first-class
physics objects.

- Task: specification of input substrate → output substrate
- Constructor: entity that can cause a task and retains ability to cause
  it again (catalyst, engine, factory, living cell)
- Possible: laws permit a constructor for it. Impossible: no constructor could.

Constructor theory of information: information medium = physical system
with distinguishable attributes where flip and copy are possible.
Superinformation media (quantum) = copy-all impossible, all transformations
reversible. Derives no-cloning, complementarity from constructor principles.

Constructor theory of life (Marletto, 2015): any laws permitting information
media must permit replicators. Replicator = system that can cause
approximate copies of itself. Under no-design laws, recipe for self-
replication must be modular, copied blindly, with error correction.

Knowledge in constructor theory: information that, once physically
instantiated in suitable environment, tends to cause itself to remain
instantiated. Survives criticism, testing, noise, error-correction.
A physical characterization of knowledge connecting to Popper.
