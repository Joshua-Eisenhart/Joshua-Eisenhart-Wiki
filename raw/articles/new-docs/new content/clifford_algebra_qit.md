## 1. Clifford Algebra Cl(p,q): Definition and Construction

### 1.1 Definition

Cl(p,q) is the real associative algebra generated by e_1, ..., e_n (n = p + q) subject to:

    e_i e_j + e_j e_i = 2 g_{ij}

where g = diag(+1, ..., +1, -1, ..., -1) with p positive and q negative entries.

Equivalently:
    e_i^2 = +1  for i = 1, ..., p
    e_i^2 = -1  for i = p+1, ..., p+q
    e_i e_j = -e_j e_i  for i =/= j

### 1.2 Basis and Dimension

Cl(p,q) has dimension 2^n. A basis is formed by all products of distinct generators:

    {1, e_1, e_2, ..., e_n, e_{12}, e_{13}, ..., e_{12...n}}

where e_{i_1 i_2 ... i_k} = e_{i_1} e_{i_2} ... e_{i_k} with i_1 < i_2 < ... < i_k.

These are graded by degree k:
- Grade 0: 1 element (scalars), C(n,0) = 1
- Grade 1: n elements (vectors)
- Grade 2: C(n,2) elements (bivectors)
- Grade k: C(n,k) elements (k-vectors)
- Grade n: 1 element (pseudoscalar)

Total: sum_{k=0}^{n} C(n,k) = 2^n.

## 2. Cl(3,0): The Algebra of Physical 3-Space

### 2.1 Generators and Relations

Three generators e_1, e_2, e_3 with:

    e_i^2 = 1  for all i
    e_i e_j = -e_j e_i  for i =/= j

### 2.2 Complete Basis (8 elements)

Grade 0: {1}                          — 1 element (scalar)
Grade 1: {e_1, e_2, e_3}             — 3 elements (vectors)
Grade 2: {e_{12}, e_{13}, e_{23}}     — 3 elements (bivectors)
Grade 3: {e_{123}}                    — 1 element (pseudoscalar)

where e_{12} = e_1 e_2, e_{13} = e_1 e_3, e_{23} = e_2 e_3, e_{123} = e_1 e_2 e_3.

### 2.3 Pseudoscalar Properties

    I = e_{123} = e_1 e_2 e_3

Properties:
    I^2 = e_1 e_2 e_3 e_1 e_2 e_3 = -e_1 e_2 e_1 e_3 e_2 e_3 = ... = -1

    (Working: e_3 e_1 = -e_1 e_3, so I^2 = e_1 e_2 e_3 e_1 e_2 e_3.
     Move e_1 past e_3: -e_1 e_2 e_1 e_3 e_2 e_3.
     Move e_1 past e_2: +e_1 e_1 e_2 e_3 e_2 e_3 = e_2 e_3 e_2 e_3.
     Move e_2 past e_3: -e_2 e_2 e_3 e_3 = -1.)

I commutes with all elements of Cl(3,0) (it is central):
    I e_k = e_1 e_2 e_3 e_k = (-1)^2 e_k e_1 e_2 e_3 = e_k I

(Each e_k anticommutes with the two other generators in I, giving (-1)^2 = +1.)

### 2.4 Duality

The pseudoscalar I establishes a duality between vectors and bivectors:

    I e_1 = e_{123} e_1 = e_{23}    (e_1 dual to e_{23})
    I e_2 = e_{123} e_2 = -e_{13}   (e_2 dual to -e_{13})
    I e_3 = e_{123} e_3 = e_{12}    (e_3 dual to e_{12})

Or: e_{ij} = I epsilon_{ijk} e_k (with appropriate signs).

This is the geometric algebra version of the cross product: a x b = -I(a wedge b).

## 3. Isomorphism Cl(3,0) = M_2(C)

### 3.1 The Map

Cl(3,0) is isomorphic (as a real algebra) to M_2(C) = the algebra of 2x2 complex matrices.

One explicit isomorphism:

    e_1 <-> sigma_x = [[0, 1], [1, 0]]
    e_2 <-> sigma_y = [[0, -i], [i, 0]]
    e_3 <-> sigma_z = [[1, 0], [0, -1]]

Check: sigma_x sigma_y = i sigma_z, and e_1 e_2 = e_{12}. So:

    e_{12} <-> i sigma_z
    e_{13} <-> -i sigma_y
    e_{23} <-> i sigma_x
    I = e_{123} <-> i sigma_x sigma_y sigma_z ... wait.

Let me compute: e_{123} <-> sigma_x sigma_y sigma_z = (i sigma_z)(sigma_z) = i I_2.

Actually: sigma_x sigma_y = i sigma_z, so sigma_x sigma_y sigma_z = i sigma_z^2 = i I.

So: e_{123} = I <-> i I_2 (i times identity matrix).

Complete map:
    1 <-> I_2 = [[1,0],[0,1]]
    e_1 <-> sigma_x
    e_2 <-> sigma_y  (note: includes a factor of i in the matrix)
    e_3 <-> sigma_z
    e_{12} <-> i sigma_z
    e_{23} <-> i sigma_x
    e_{13} <-> -i sigma_y
    e_{123} <-> i I_2

### 3.2 Dimension Check

Cl(3,0) has dimension 8 over R. M_2(C) has dimension 8 over R (4 complex entries = 8 real parameters). Check.

### 3.3 Even Subalgebra

The even subalgebra Cl^+(3,0) consists of elements of even grade: {1, e_{12}, e_{13}, e_{23}}.

Under the isomorphism: {I_2, i sigma_z, -i sigma_y, i sigma_x} = span_R {I_2, i sigma_x, i sigma_y, i sigma_z}.

This is exactly the algebra of quaternions H, which is isomorphic to su(2) as a Lie algebra (the i sigma_k being the standard basis of su(2)).

## 4. Rotors and SU(2) = Spin(3)

### 4.1 Rotors

A rotor R in Cl(3,0) is an element of the even subalgebra satisfying R R_tilde = 1, where R_tilde is the reverse (reverse the order of all geometric products).

General rotor:

    R = exp(-theta B/2)

where B is a unit bivector (B^2 = -1) and theta is the rotation angle.

The rotor acts on vectors by:

    v -> R v R_tilde

This is a rotation by angle theta in the plane of B.

### 4.2 Bivector = Rotation Plane

    B = e_{12}: rotation in the (e_1, e_2) plane = rotation about e_3 axis
    B = e_{23}: rotation in the (e_2, e_3) plane = rotation about e_1 axis
    B = e_{13}: rotation in the (e_1, e_3) plane = rotation about e_2 axis

General unit bivector: B = n_1 e_{23} + n_2 e_{13} + n_3 e_{12} with |n| = 1.

### 4.3 Double Cover

R and -R give the same rotation (since v -> R v R_tilde = (-R) v (-R_tilde)). The group of rotors is Spin(3) = SU(2), which double-covers SO(3).

Under the isomorphism:

    R = exp(-theta B/2) <-> exp(-i theta n . sigma / 2) in SU(2)

### 4.4 Explicit Rotor Form

    R = cos(theta/2) - sin(theta/2) B

For B = e_{12} (rotation about e_3):
    R = cos(theta/2) - sin(theta/2) e_{12}
    <-> cos(theta/2) I_2 - i sin(theta/2) sigma_z
    = [[e^{-i theta/2}, 0], [0, e^{i theta/2}]]

This is the standard SU(2) matrix for z-rotation.

## 5. Single Qubit in Geometric Algebra

### 5.1 State as Rotor

A pure qubit state |psi> can be represented as a rotor psi in Cl(3,0):

    |psi> = psi |0>  (rotor acting on reference state)

where |0> corresponds to the "spin-up" direction e_3.

The density matrix becomes:

    rho = (1/2)(1 + n . sigma) <-> (1/2)(1 + m) in Cl(3,0)

where m = psi e_3 psi_tilde is the Bloch vector (a vector in Cl(3,0)).

### 5.2 Expectation Values

For observable A = a . sigma <-> a (a vector in Cl(3,0)):

    <A> = Tr(rho A) <-> <rho A>_0 = (1/2)<(1 + m) a>_0 = (1/2) m . a

where <>_0 denotes the scalar (grade-0) part and m . a = (1/2)(ma + am) is the inner product.

## 6. Cl(6,0) for Two-Qubit Systems

### 6.1 Construction

For two qubits, use Cl(6,0) with generators e_1, ..., e_6.

Assignment:
- System A: e_1, e_2, e_3 (first qubit Pauli operators)
- System B: e_4, e_5, e_6 (second qubit Pauli operators)

Since all generators anticommute: e_i e_j = -e_j e_i for all i =/= j.

### 6.2 Bivector Classification

The 15 bivectors of Cl(6,0) decompose as:

**Local bivectors of A**: e_{12}, e_{13}, e_{23} (3 bivectors)
These generate rotations within subsystem A.

**Local bivectors of B**: e_{45}, e_{46}, e_{56} (3 bivectors)
These generate rotations within subsystem B.

**Cross-bivectors**: e_{14}, e_{15}, e_{16}, e_{24}, e_{25}, e_{26}, e_{34}, e_{35}, e_{36} (9 bivectors)
These mix the two subsystems and encode correlations/entanglement.

### 6.3 Cross-Bivectors and Entanglement

The 9 cross-bivectors e_{ia} (i in {1,2,3}, a in {4,5,6}) correspond to the 9 components of the correlation tensor T_{ij}.

Under the isomorphism to M_4(C):

    e_{ia} <-> sigma_i tensor sigma_{a-3}  (up to phases from the anticommutation)

**CRITICAL SUBTLETY**: The geometric product in Cl(6) and the tensor product in C^2 tensor C^2 do NOT agree directly, because in Cl(6) all generators anticommute, while in the tensor product, operators on different subsystems commute.

Specifically: in QM, sigma_x tensor I and I tensor sigma_z commute. But in Cl(6), e_1 and e_6 anticommute.

### 6.4 Reconciling Geometric Product and Tensor Product

The fix: Use a twisted embedding. Define:

    Sigma_1 = e_1,  Sigma_2 = e_2,  Sigma_3 = e_3  (system A, straightforward)

For system B, absorb the A-pseudoscalar:

    Sigma_4 = I_A e_4 = e_{123} e_4 = e_{1234}
    Sigma_5 = I_A e_5 = e_{1235}
    Sigma_6 = I_A e_6 = e_{1236}

Now: Sigma_i Sigma_a = e_i (e_{123} e_a) = e_{123} (e_i e_a) ... and since I_A commutes with e_i (in Cl(3)):

    Sigma_1 Sigma_4 = e_1 e_{1234} = e_{234}
    Sigma_4 Sigma_1 = e_{1234} e_1 = e_{234}  (since e_{1234} e_1 = e_1 e_{1234} for even-grade element times e_1... 

Actually let me compute: e_{1234} e_1 = e_1 e_2 e_3 e_4 e_1 = -e_1 e_2 e_3 e_1 e_4 = ... = e_2 e_3 e_4 = e_{234}.

And e_1 e_{1234} = e_1 e_1 e_2 e_3 e_4 = e_{234}.

So Sigma_1 Sigma_4 = Sigma_4 Sigma_1. They COMMUTE, as desired for tensor product structure.

This twisted embedding maps:
    sigma_i tensor I <-> Sigma_i  (grade 1 in A)
    I tensor sigma_j <-> Sigma_{j+3}  (grade 3 or 4 in the full algebra)
    sigma_i tensor sigma_j <-> Sigma_i Sigma_{j+3}  (various grades)

And [Sigma_i, Sigma_{j+3}] = 0 for all i, j — recovering the tensor product commutation relations.

## 7. Entanglement in the Clifford Framework

### 7.1 Entanglement Bivector

Define the entanglement bivector:

    E = sum_{i,j} T_{ij} Sigma_i Sigma_{j+3}

where T_{ij} is the correlation tensor. The SVD of T gives the principal correlations.

For a maximally entangled state (Bell state): T is orthogonal (T^T T = I), and E has maximum "area" in the 6D space.

### 7.2 Concurrence from Geometric Algebra

The concurrence for a 2-qubit pure state |Psi> can be expressed as:

    C = |<Psi| sigma_y tensor sigma_y |Psi*>|

In the Clifford framework, sigma_y tensor sigma_y corresponds to e_2 e_5 (or its twisted version). The concurrence measures the "component" of the state in the maximally entangled subspace.

### 7.3 SLOCC Classification via Cl(6)

Stochastic local operations and classical communication (SLOCC) equivalence classes correspond to orbits of SL(2,C) x SL(2,C) acting on C^4.

In the Clifford framework, this group embeds into Cl(6) as:

    SL(2,C) x SL(2,C) subset Spin(6, C)

The invariant theory of Spin(6) acting on the spinor representation classifies entanglement.

## 8. Geometric Product: Detailed Properties

### 8.1 Geometric Product of Vectors

For vectors a, b in Cl(p,q):

    a b = a . b + a wedge b

where:
    a . b = (1/2)(a b + b a) = scalar (grade 0) — inner product
    a wedge b = (1/2)(a b - b a) = bivector (grade 2) — outer product

This decomposition only holds for grade-1 elements. For general multivectors, the geometric product is more complex.

### 8.2 Multivector Products

For an r-vector A_r and s-vector B_s:

    A_r B_s = <A_r B_s>_{|r-s|} + <A_r B_s>_{|r-s|+2} + ... + <A_r B_s>_{r+s}

where <>_k denotes the grade-k part.

The inner product (grade-lowering): A_r . B_s = <A_r B_s>_{|r-s|}
The outer product (grade-raising): A_r wedge B_s = <A_r B_s>_{r+s}

### 8.3 Reversion

For a product of vectors: (a_1 a_2 ... a_k)_tilde = a_k ... a_2 a_1.

On grade-k elements: A_tilde_k = (-1)^{k(k-1)/2} A_k.

So: scalars and vectors are self-reverse, bivectors and pseudoscalars flip sign in Cl(3,0).

## 9. Cl(1,3) vs Cl(3,1): Spacetime Algebras

### 9.1 Cl(1,3) — Dirac Algebra (West Coast Convention)

Generators gamma_0, gamma_1, gamma_2, gamma_3 with gamma_0^2 = +1, gamma_i^2 = -1.

Isomorphic to M_4(R) — 4x4 real matrices.

The Dirac equation in geometric algebra: nabla psi I sigma_3 = m psi gamma_0.

### 9.2 Cl(3,1) — (East Coast Convention)

Generators with gamma_0^2 = -1, gamma_i^2 = +1.

Isomorphic to M_2(H) — 2x2 quaternionic matrices.

### 9.3 Relevance to QIT

The spacetime algebra is relevant when considering:
- Relativistic quantum information
- Unruh effect and entanglement
- Causal structure constraints on quantum channels
- CPT symmetries of quantum channels

The geometric algebra formulation makes the Lorentz covariance manifest without needing to introduce Dirac spinors as column vectors.

## 10. Pin and Spin Groups

### 10.1 Definitions

**Pin(p,q)**: Group of products of unit vectors in Cl(p,q). Acts on vectors by v -> s v s_tilde (possibly including reflections and rotations).

**Spin(p,q)**: Even subgroup of Pin(p,q) (products of even numbers of unit vectors). Acts by rotations only.

**Spin(3) = SU(2)**: Double cover of SO(3). Generated by even elements of Cl(3,0).

**Spin(4) = SU(2) x SU(2)**: Double cover of SO(4). Relevant to 2-qubit local unitaries.

**Spin(6) = SU(4)**: Double cover of SO(6). Relevant to 2-qubit arbitrary unitaries. The isomorphism Spin(6) = SU(4) relates the 15 generators of the Lie algebra:
- 6 bivectors of Cl(6) in the defining representation = 15 generators of su(4) in the spinor representation.
- This is the "accidental isomorphism" that connects the geometry of R^6 to 2-qubit quantum gates.

### 10.2 Accidental Isomorphisms Relevant to QIT

    Spin(2) = U(1)           — phase gates
    Spin(3) = SU(2) = Sp(1)  — 1-qubit gates
    Spin(4) = SU(2) x SU(2)  — local 2-qubit gates
    Spin(5) = Sp(2)           — ...
    Spin(6) = SU(4)           — all 2-qubit gates

These stop at Spin(6); for n >= 7, Spin(n) is not isomorphic to any classical matrix group.

This means 2 qubits is the LAST case where the full gate group has a simple Clifford algebra description via an accidental isomorphism. For 3+ qubits, the structure is fundamentally more complex.
