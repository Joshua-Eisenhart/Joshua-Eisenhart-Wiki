## Sim 1: Does the engine's symmetry structure map onto gauge groups?

**From mismatch:** "Standard model's particle zoo reflecting QIT engine
attractor basin" — max falsifiable claim, no specific mapping exists yet.

**What to test:** Does the engine's constraint-surviving symmetry structure
(the operators Ti/Fe/Te/Fi, their commutation relations, and the
Type1/Type2 chirality) produce symmetry groups that correspond to any
subset of SU(3)×SU(2)×U(1)?

**Sim spec:**
- Input: the 4 operators as 2×2 matrices from geometric_operators.py
- Compute: the Lie algebra generated by commutators [Ti,Fe], [Ti,Te],
  [Fe,Fi], etc. All 6 pairwise commutators of 4 operators.
- Compute: the dimension of the generated Lie algebra
- Compute: whether it closes (finite-dimensional) or not
- Compare: does the algebra match u(1), su(2), su(3), or any subalgebra
  of su(3)×su(2)×u(1)?
- Kill condition: if the generated algebra has no structural correspondence
  to any standard model gauge subalgebra, the claim is killed at this level

**Resolution level:** Geometric (operators)
**Boot type:** A1/recon (this is mapping the candidate, not formal ratchet)
**Expected output:** JSON with algebra dimension, closure status,
identified subalgebras (if any)

## Sim 2: Is viability kernel the right frame (not attractor basin)?

**From mismatch:** "System explicitly rejects attractor-pull language.
Viability asks 'can it stay?' not 'where does it go?'"

**What to test:** Does the engine's behavior match viability kernel
dynamics (survival under constraint) or attractor basin dynamics
(convergence to a fixed point/cycle)?

**Sim spec:**
- Run engine from 20 different initial conditions (varying eta, theta1, theta2)
- For each: run 10 full cycles (80 stages)
- Track: does the trajectory converge to a fixed point/cycle (attractor)?
  Or does it remain within a region without converging (viability)?
- Measure: trajectory spread after 10 cycles. If it narrows → attractor.
  If it stays bounded but doesn't narrow → viability.
- Also: perturb mid-cycle and check if trajectory returns to same path
  (attractor — robust return) or stays within bounds but on a different
  path (viability — multiple viable paths)
- Kill condition: if all trajectories converge to the same fixed point
  regardless of initial condition, the viability framing is wrong and
  attractor basin is the correct description

**Resolution level:** Geometric (engine dynamics)
**Boot type:** B/ratchet (this is a constraint check, not advocacy)
**Expected output:** JSON with convergence metrics, trajectory spread,
perturbation response, verdict (attractor vs viability vs inconclusive)

## Sim 3: Does Čencov uniqueness hold or does the quantum non-uniqueness matter?

**From mismatch:** "Fisher-Rao metric: Strong fit. System may need quantum
version (Petz: infinitely many metrics, not unique)"

**What to test:** The system operates on 2×2 and 4×4 density matrices
(quantum objects). Čencov's theorem gives a UNIQUE metric classically.
Petz's theorem gives INFINITELY MANY monotone metrics quantumly. Does
the system's behavior depend on which quantum metric is chosen?

**Sim spec:**
- Take the engine's rho_AB at each stage of a full cycle
- Compute distances between consecutive stages using three different
  quantum metrics:
  1. Bures metric (smallest monotone metric — SLD Fisher info)
  2. Wigner-Yanase metric (middle)
  3. Kubo-Mori metric (largest monotone metric)
- Compare: do the three metrics give the same ORDERING of stage
  distances? (i.e., is the geometry qualitatively the same?)
- If yes: the non-uniqueness doesn't matter for this system at this
  resolution. Čencov-like behavior holds effectively.
- If no: the system needs to specify WHICH quantum metric it uses,
  and the choice is a structural commitment not yet made.
- Kill condition: if the three metrics give qualitatively different
  geometries (different ordering of which stages are "near" vs "far"),
  then the system must address quantum metric non-uniqueness explicitly

**Resolution level:** Geometric (density matrix geometry)
**Boot type:** A1/recon (exploring the candidate's geometric structure)
**Expected output:** JSON with distance matrices under each metric,
ordering comparison, qualitative agreement verdict

## Why These Three

These are the three most falsifiable mismatches from the tradition mapping:

1. **Gauge group correspondence** — the owner's strongest claim (engine =
   universal pattern), tested at its most specific (does the math match
   the standard model?). Maximum Popper pressure.

2. **Viability vs attractor** — tests the fundamental framing of the
   system (constraint survival vs convergence). If the engine behaves as
   an attractor, the entire process doc's framing needs revision.

3. **Metric uniqueness** — tests whether classical intuitions (unique
   geometry of distinguishability) survive at the quantum level where
   the engine actually operates. If they don't, the system needs
   additional structural commitments.

Each sim is designed to KILL a specific claim. If the claim survives,
that's informative. If it dies, that's MORE informative — it maps the
boundary of M(C).
