What is Delegated Proxy Voting?
Delegated proxy voting is a way for people to choose someone they trust to vote on their behalf, like picking a leader to make decisions for them. It’s not about everyone voting equally; instead, it’s about giving your vote to someone better at making choices, creating a hierarchy based on ability.

You can assign your vote to anyone, and you can change your mind anytime, keeping things flexible. For example, if you trust a community expert, you might let them vote for you on local projects. Their decisions are logged transparently, so you can see what they do, ensuring they’re accountable.
Why Use It?

Mechanics of Delegated Proxy Voting
Delegated proxy voting is a fluid, hierarchical process where participants voluntarily assign their voting power to trusted delegates, who may further delegate upward through multiple layers, culminating in a single leader responsible for solutions, laws, leadership, and management. This system rejects rule by committee or equity-based decision-making, emphasizing centralized accountability through a dynamic, merit-based hierarchy. The mechanics leverage the Leviathan OS and blockchain technology to ensure transparency, flexibility, and empirical outcomes, aligning with the framework’s anarcho-capitalist principles.
Delegation Process
The delegation process is structured to create a clear, merit-based hierarchy while maintaining flexibility and accountability:
Initial Delegation: Each individual assigns their vote to a trusted delegate, such as a respected community member, cooperative leader, or organizational expert. This choice is voluntary, reflecting high agency and self-reflection as participants assess delegates’ competence based on their performance, validated through the Ugly Mirror’s metrics or empirical outcomes. For example, a cooperative member might delegate their vote to a leader with a high social credit score for managing community projects effectively.
Layered Delegation: Delegates can further assign their aggregated voting power to another delegate at a higher layer, creating a multi-layered hierarchy. Each layer consolidates authority, with higher layers wielding greater influence due to accumulated votes. For instance, in a cooperative with 100 members, each member delegates to one of five local leaders, who then delegate to a cooperative president, who may further delegate to a regional leader in a larger network. This creates a clear chain of authority, with power increasing at each layer.
Single Apex Leader: The delegation process culminates in a single leader at the top, responsible for all solutions, laws, leadership, and management. This leader is selected through successive layers of delegation, ensuring only the most competent individual, validated by performance, holds ultimate authority. For example, a global cooperative network might delegate to a CEO-like leader who oversees trade policies, chosen for their proven track record.
Dynamic Adjustments: Delegations are revocable and fluid, allowing participants to reassign votes if a delegate underperforms or loses trust. This ensures recursive growth cycles, as participants can shift support to more effective leaders based on empirical feedback, such as policy outcomes logged on blockchain.
This process creates a hierarchy where power is concentrated at the top but derived from voluntary trust at the base, ensuring both accountability and efficiency.
